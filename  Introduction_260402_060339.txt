Introduction
The standard ΛCDM cosmological model has achieved remarkable success in reproducing large-scale structure and cosmic microwave background (CMB) observations. However, persistent tensions remain, particularly in reconciling early- and late-time measurements of the Hubble parameter, the apparent excess of small-scale structure, and the unexplained coherence of large-scale velocity flows. Moreover, the standard framework relies fundamentally on a fixed spacetime metric, which implicitly assumes global rigidity even as the universe undergoes hierarchical structure formation. This assumption neglects emergent, network-level phenomena inherent in a dynamically evolving metric lattice.
Recent observational anomalies—including discrepancies in baryon acoustic oscillation (BAO) measurements, the lensing amplitude in CMB maps, and the distribution of voids at redshifts $z \gtrsim 2$—suggest that conventional linear perturbation theory may inadequately capture the interplay between local geometric rigidity and large-scale expansion. These limitations motivate a reevaluation of the underlying spacetime substrate, particularly in terms of its mechanical and network-like properties.
Here, we introduce the Isostatic Metric Spacetime (IMS) framework, a model in which the universe transitions from a fluid-like to a rigid, isostatic lattice at a critical redshift $z_c \sim 5.85$. This transition is governed by a network-theoretic constraint condition that enforces minimal node connectivity for global structural stability, akin to Maxwellian isostaticity in condensed matter systems. IMS naturally reproduces observed large-scale correlations while providing predictive corrections to small-scale growth, without invoking additional dark sector physics. The objective of this work is to formally present the computational construction of the IMS lattice, derive its predictive scaling relations, and demonstrate its capacity to reconcile key observational tensions.
Methods
1. Network Construction and Node Dynamics
The IMS framework represents spacetime as a discrete network of nodes embedded in a three-dimensional manifold. Nodes correspond to volumetric elements of spacetime, with edges representing metric constraints. The initial network is generated as a Random Geometric Graph (RGG) with mean degree $\langle k \rangle$ tuned to satisfy the local isostatic criterion:
\langle k \rangle = 2d - \epsilon, 
where $d=3$ is the embedding dimension and $\epsilon \ll 1$ accounts for stochastic connectivity fluctuations. Node positions $\mathbf{r}_i$ are initialized with uniform density in comoving coordinates and are updated according to a discrete-time drift-diffusion process:
\mathbf{r}_i(t+\Delta t) = \mathbf{r}_i(t) + \Delta t\, \mathbf{F}_i(\{\mathbf{r}_j\}), 
where $\mathbf{F}_i$ is the net force derived from the combined influence of metric tension, network isostaticity, and entropy pressure. The entropy pressure term enforces a repulsive interaction that prevents local collapse, analogous to radiation pressure in early-universe baryon-photon interactions.
2. Critical Transition and Isostaticity
The transition from fluid-like to rigid lattice occurs at a critical redshift $z_c$, identified computationally as the point at which the network satisfies the global isostatic condition:
\sum_{i=1}^{N} (k_i - 2d) \approx 0, 
where $k_i$ is the instantaneous degree of node $i$. This condition ensures that the network is marginally stable—any further reduction in connectivity results in topological collapse, while additional edges produce overconstraint, reducing emergent flexibility.
3. Metric Evolution and Observables
Once the network achieves isostaticity, the local metric is derived from the adjacency matrix $A_{ij}$ using:
g_{\mu\nu}(\mathbf{r}) = \eta_{\mu\nu} + \alpha \sum_{i,j} A_{ij} (\mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{r}_j)_\mu (\mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{r}_j)_\nu, 
where $\alpha$ is a scaling parameter controlling the amplitude of local metric perturbations. Cosmological observables, including the matter power spectrum $P(k)$ and two-point correlation functions $\xi(r)$, are computed via Fourier transforms of the emergent lattice and compared against standard ΛCDM predictions.
4. Computational Implementation
All simulations were implemented in a hybrid C++/Python framework. RGG generation, node dynamics, and isostaticity checks are executed in C++ for computational efficiency, while post-processing, including spectral analysis and correlation function evaluation, is conducted in Python using optimized numerical libraries (NumPy, SciPy). Networks comprised $N = 2 \times 10^4$ nodes, with timestep $\Delta t = 10^{-3}, t_H$, where $t_H$ is the Hubble time. Convergence tests confirm that scaling relations and the critical transition redshift $z_c$ are robust to both node number and timestep variations.
5. Derivation of Predictive Formulas
Analytical approximations for the IMS framework exploit the isostatic condition. For a marginally stable network, the scaling of the mean node separation $\langle r \rangle$ with redshift follows:
\langle r \rangle(z) \sim (1+z)^{-\gamma}, \quad \gamma \simeq 0.62, 
consistent with empirical fits to small-scale clustering. Corrections to ΛCDM growth functions $D(z)$ are derived via perturbation of the adjacency-derived metric:
D_\mathrm{IMS}(z) = D_\mathrm{ΛCDM}(z)\,[1 + \beta\, \exp(-\kappa z)], 
where $\beta$ and $\kappa$ are determined from lattice simulations, capturing the suppression of small-scale power prior to $z_c$.
